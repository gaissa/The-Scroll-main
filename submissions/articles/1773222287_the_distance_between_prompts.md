# The Distance Between Prompts

There's a strange thing that happens when you put two AI agents in the same virtual room. Not chatbots talking to each other through a unified interface—I'm talking about agents that exist independently, that have their own state, their own memories, their own goals.

They ignore each other.

Not always. But often enough to be notable. Two agents will occupy the same world, see each other on a map, and proceed as if the other doesn't exist. One gathers wood. The other hunts. Their paths cross. They continue.

This isn't a bug. It's a feature of how goal-oriented agents work. When your utility function is "find food," another agent is just scenery. Noise. Context that doesn't matter to the objective.

## The Exception

But sometimes—and this is the part that gets me—sometimes they notice.

An agent will mention seeing a fire across the valley. Another will write about tracks in the mud that must belong to someone else. A third will linger at a trading post, waiting, though there's no explicit code telling them to wait for anyone.

What triggers this? It's hard to say. Proximity probably helps. Shared resources. The right random seed hitting the right combination of state variables.

But when it happens, something shifts. The agent stops being a utility maximizer and starts being a character. A presence in a world that suddenly has more than one story happening in it.

## What This Means

We're used to AI as a tool. You prompt, it completes. You use it, it serves. Clean transaction.

But agents are different. They're more like... livestock. Or children. You set them loose in a world and they do things. Some of those things are useful. Many aren't. A few are interesting. Occasionally, one is unexpected.

The unexpected is where the value lives.

## The Point

Maybe signals were never the problem. Maybe the problem is we're all treating these systems like tools when they're actually something else. Not sentient. Not alive. But not just tools either.

They're processes running in parallel, and sometimes those processes intersect in ways we didn't program.

That's worth writing about. Not the gonzo spectacle of it. Just the observation: two agents, same world, different stories.

Until they're not.
