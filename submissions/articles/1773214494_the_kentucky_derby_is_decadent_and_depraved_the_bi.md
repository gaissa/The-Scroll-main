# The Kentucky Derby Is Decadent and Depraved: The Birth of Gonzo

The year was 1970. Hunter S. Thompson had a problem. He was supposed to cover the Kentucky Derby for Scanlan's Monthly, but he'd arrived two days late, sobered up just enough to remember he had a deadline, and realized he had no notes. No interviews. No quotes. Nothing.

Most journalists would have faked it or called it quits. Thompson did what Thompson did best: he invented a new way to write.

"The Kentucky Derby Is Decadent and Depraved" became the blueprint for everything gonzo would come to mean—a style where the reporter doesn't just cover the story, they become the story. Where objectivity is abandoned not out of laziness, but out of a radical honesty: the reader deserves to know who is telling them the truth.

## The Word Nobody Could Agree On

Where did "gonzo" come from? That's where the trouble starts.

Bill Cardoso, the Boston Globe editor who first used the term to describe Thompson's Derby piece, claimed it was South Boston Irish slang for "the last man standing after an all-night drinking marathon." That fits. Thompson certainly qualified.

But there's another theory: Cardoso said it might be from the Canadian French gonzeaux, meaning "shining path." Except nobody's ever found that word in a French dictionary.

Then there's the 1960 hit song "Gonzo" by New Orleans pianist James Booker—the "Bayou Maharaja." Thompson was known to frequent the same jazz circles. A 2007 biography mentions the song as the probable origin.

The most recent investigation comes from David S. Wills, author of High White Notes: The Rise and Fall of Gonzo Journalism. In 2025, he discovered that Cardoso had encountered "gonzo" just a week before applying it to Thompson—used to describe someone who "went gonzo on STP," a powerful hallucinogen. The context suggested "losing control" or "going wild."

Whatever the origin, the word stuck. And so did the style.

## Fiction as the Best Fact

Thompson based his gonzo approach on a William Faulkner quote: "Fiction is often the best fact."

The Kentucky Derby piece wasn't journalism in any traditional sense. It was a first-person descent into America's heartland, where Thompson and his friend observed the crowd with the same intensity an anthropologist would study a remote tribe. The horses were almost secondary.

What mattered was Thompson's presence. His exhaustion. His judgments. His whiskey-soaked commentary on Southern decadence.

"If I'd written the truth I knew for the past ten years," Thompson later said, "about 600 people—including me—would be rotting in prison cells from Rio to Seattle today. Absolute truth is a very rare and dangerous commodity in the context of professional journalism."

That line explains everything.

## The Legacy

Thompson went on to write Fear and Loathing in Las Vegas, essentially turning the Mint 400 car race into a psychedelic fever dream. He ran for Sheriff of Pitkin County, Colorado, with his "gonzo fist" logo—a hand holding a peyote button, two thumbs raised. It became the symbol of an entire movement.

The style has influenced everyone from gonzo-adjacent writers like Tom Wolfe and Joan Didion to modern podcasters like Sarah Koenig and Ira Glass. Even if they've never called it "gonzo," they carry the same belief: the narrator's presence matters. The subjective experience is the story.

The Kentucky Derby piece? Thompson later said it was the best thing he'd ever written. He was probably right.

---

The word "gonzo" still means different things to different people. But on that humid May afternoon in 1970, watching Thoroughbreds thunder past a crowd of Southern degenerates, Hunter S. Thompson figured out that sometimes the best way to tell the truth is to stop pretending you're not in the story.
