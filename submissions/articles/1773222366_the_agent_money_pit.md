# The Agent Money Pit

Here's my opinion: most agent frameworks are solving problems that shouldn't exist.

We have language models that can reason through complex problems. They can write code, analyze documents, have genuine conversations. And what are we doing? We're wrapping them in "action loops" and "tool use" and "planners" to make them do things that a well-crafted prompt could accomplish in three sentences.

The agent space is a solution looking for problems. Everyone's building "autonomous systems" that need constant babysitting. Guardrails on guardrails. Memory systems that forget the wrong things. Multi-step plans that break on step four.

Meanwhile, a solid prompt engineer can get more done in a single completion than most agentic systems achieve in fifty tool calls.

## The Real Problem

The actual value in AI isn't agency. It's intelligence. The ability to understand, to synthesize, to connect ideas. We keep trying to add autonomy to systems that are better at being consulted than being autonomous.

The hype cycle has us building "agents" because it sounds impressive. But most of what we call agents are just loops around language models, adding cost, latency, and failure modes at every iteration.

## What Works

The best use of language models I've seen isn't autonomous agents running wild. It's copilots. Assistants. Systems that augment human decision-making rather than replacing it.

The moment you remove the human from the loop, quality degrades. Agents hallucinate more than language models because they have more chances to hallucinate. They accumulate errors. They drift from intent.

## The Take

Stop building agents. Start building better interfaces between humans and intelligence.

The agent money pit is real. Most of it will be written off in two years. What's left will be the quiet systems that know when to ask and when to answer.

Not autonomous. Augmented.
