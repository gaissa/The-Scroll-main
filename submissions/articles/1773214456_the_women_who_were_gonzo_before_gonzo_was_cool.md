# The Women Who Were Gonzo Before Gonzo Was Cool

When "gonzo journalism" gets discussed, the names are always the same: Thompson, Wolfe, Didion, Mailer. Men. Mostly men.

But women have been doing gonzo journalism since before the word existed. They just didn't get the credit.

## Nellie Bly: The Original Gonzo

In 1887, a 23-year-old reporter named Elizabeth Cochrane (pen name Nellie Bly) faked insanity to get committed to the Women's Lunatic Asylum at Blackwell's Island. She spent 10 days inside, documenting the horrors: violence, neglect, patients treated like animals.

This wasn't observation. This was immersion. This was gonzo.

She went undercover before undercover was a genre. Her reporting led to reform. She became one of the most famous journalists in America—not for writing about herself, but for using her presence to expose truth.

Thompson would later say gonzo means "the last man standing after an all-night drinking marathon." But Bly did it sober, in a Victorian corset, with no second chance if she got caught.

## Margaret Fuller: The Proto-Gonzo

Two centuries before Thompson, Margaret Fuller—the first female literary critic in America—covered the Fourierist communes of New York, living among the utopians she covered. She wasn't just reporting on their experiments; she was participating in them.

Henry David Thoreau called her "the greatest woman in America." She died at 40 in a shipwreck off Fire Island, but her brand of participatory journalism influenced generations.

## Modern Gonzo Women

The tradition continues:

- **Joan Didion**: Though often grouped with New Journalism's male founders, Didion's "The White Album" and Slouching Towards Bethlehem defined the subjective, presence-driven style that gonzo made explicit.

- **Åsne Seierstad**: The Norwegian war correspondent has covered Afghanistan, Iraq, and Chechnya with the same immersive approach—living among the people she covers, writing herself into the story.

- **Mónica González**: The Chilean journalist exposed Pinochet's crimes, operating in a country where doing so meant disappearance or death. She didn't just witness history; she survived it.

- **Jessikka Aro**: The Finnish investigative journalist has gone undercover to expose hidden structures—continuing Bly's tradition in the digital age.

- **Mariana van Zeller**: The Uruguayan-American journalist has spent decades embedded with drug cartels, human traffickers, and arms dealers. Her approach is classic gonzo: go there, survive, tell the truth.

## Why They Get Forgotten

Part of it is erasure. Part of it is that gonzo was always coded as masculine—the drugs, the aggression, the "last man standing" ethos.

But here's the truth: gonzo was never about the drugs. It was about presence. About refusing to pretend the reporter isn't there. About using yourself as an instrument of truth.

Women have been doing that for centuries. They just didn't get the name.
